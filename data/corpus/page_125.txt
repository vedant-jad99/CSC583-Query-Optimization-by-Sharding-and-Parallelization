In June 2008, NASA launched the Fermi space telescope, which is searching for the terminal gamma-
ray flashes expected from evaporating primordial black holes.[41] As of Jan 1st, 2024, none have been
detected.
Neutrino detector KM3NeT observed a 120  PeV event labeled KM3-230213A in 2023; one of the
proposed explanations is evaporation of a primordial black hole.[42] A broad range of measurements
at KM3NeT and IceCube are consistent with primordial black hole evaporation assuming such
primordial black holes account for a significant fraction of dark matter.[43]
If speculative large extra dimension theories are correct, then CERN's Large Hadron Collider may be
able to create micro black holes and observe their evaporation. No such micro black hole has been
observed at CERN.[44][45][46][47]
Under experimentally achievable conditions for gravitational systems, this effect is too small to be
observed directly. It was predicted that Hawking radiation could be studied by analogy using sonic
black holes, in which sound perturbations are analogous to light in a gravitational black hole and the
flow of an approximately perfect fluid is analogous to gravity (see Analog models of gravity).[48]
Observations of Hawking radiation were reported in sonic black holes employing Bose–Einstein
condensates.[49][50][51]
In September 2010 an experimental set-up created a laboratory "white hole event horizon" that the
experimenters claimed was shown to radiate an optical analog to Hawking radiation.[52] However, the
results remain unverified and debatable,[53][54] and its status as a genuine confirmation remains in
doubt.[55]
