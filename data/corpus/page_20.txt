Einstein refused surgery, saying, "I want to go when I want. It is tasteless to prolong life artificially. I
have done my share; it is time to go. I will do it elegantly."[214] He died in the Princeton Hospital early
the next morning at the age of 76, having continued to work until near the end.[215]
During the autopsy, the pathologist Thomas Stoltz Harvey removed Einstein's brain for preservation
without the permission of his family, in the hope that the neuroscience of the future would be able to
discover what made Einstein so intelligent.[216] Einstein's remains were cremated in Trenton, New
Jersey,[217] and his ashes were scattered at an undisclosed location.[218][219]
In a memorial lecture delivered on 13 December 1965 at UNESCO headquarters, nuclear physicist J.
Robert Oppenheimer summarized his impression of Einstein as a person: "He was almost wholly
without sophistication and wholly without worldliness  ... There was always with him a wonderful
purity at once childlike and profoundly stubborn."[220]
Einstein bequeathed his personal archives, library, and intellectual assets to the Hebrew University of
Jerusalem in Israel.[221]
Throughout his life, Einstein published hundreds of books and articles.[16][222] He published more
than 300 scientific papers and 150 non-scientific ones.[11][222] On 5 December 2014, universities and
archives announced the release of Einstein's papers, comprising more than 30,000 unique
documents.[223][224] In addition to the work he did by himself, he also collaborated with other
scientists on additional projects, including the Bose–Einstein statistics, the Einstein refrigerator and
others.[225][226]
Einstein's first paper,[77][227] submitted in 1900 to Annalen der Physik, was on capillary attraction. It
was published in 1901 with the title "Folgerungen aus den Capillaritätserscheinungen", which
translates as "Conclusions from the capillarity phenomena". Two papers he published in 1902–1903
(thermodynamics) attempted to interpret atomic phenomena from a statistical point of view. These
papers were the foundation for the 1905 paper on Brownian motion, which showed that Brownian
movement can be construed as firm evidence that molecules exist. His research in 1903 and 1904 was
mainly concerned with the effect of finite atomic size on diffusion phenomena.[227]
